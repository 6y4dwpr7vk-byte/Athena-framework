Worked Examples

Applied boundary analysis across institutional contexts

Overview

This section presents detailed application of the Judgment Boundary Framework across diverse institutional contexts. Each example demonstrates systematic boundary analysis, application of the classification system, and development of evidence-based conclusions.

Examples are drawn from real institutional contexts, with identifying details anonymized where appropriate to protect institutional privacy while maintaining analytical utility.

Example 1: Academic Peer Review System

Context

A university department implements a peer review process for promotion and tenure decisions. The process involves evaluation of research, teaching, and service contributions according to documented criteria.

Boundary Analysis

Stated Boundaries: The peer review committee evaluates candidates against established criteria in three domains. Committee authority extends to assessment of work quality and fit with departmental standards. Final decisions rest with administrative leadership following committee recommendations.

Observed Behaviors: Over a three-year period, the committee consistently evaluated research and teaching contributions according to documented criteria. However, in several cases, committee discussions extended to speculation about candidates' future research directions and potential productivity—matters outside the committee's competence to judge with reliability.

In one case, the committee declined to recommend a candidate who met all formal criteria, citing concerns about the candidate's "commitment to the institution" based on informal conversations rather than documented behavior. This represents substitution of subjective judgment for established criteria.

Classification

Primary Classification: Class B (Boundary-Ambiguous)
Sub-classifications: B2 (Inconsistent Application), C1 (Scope Overreach) in isolated cases
Severity: Moderate
Frequency: Occasional

Recommendations

  • Clarify that speculation about future performance falls outside committee competence
  • Limit evaluation to documented evidence and established criteria
  • Implement procedural checks requiring explicit citation of criteria for negative recommendations
  • Provide committee training on boundary maintenance and criterion application

Example 2: Healthcare Authorization System

Context

An insurance company implements a prior authorization system for specialized medical treatments. The stated purpose is verification that proposed treatments meet medical necessity criteria established in policy documents.

Boundary Analysis

Stated Boundaries: Authorization reviewers assess whether proposed treatments align with documented medical necessity criteria. Treating physicians determine appropriate treatment based on clinical judgment; reviewers verify alignment with coverage policies.

Observed Behaviors: Analysis of authorization patterns revealed systematic denial of treatments that met documented medical necessity criteria. When patterns were questioned, the company introduced new "utilization management guidelines" not previously disclosed to physicians or patients.

This represents criterion shifting—modification of standards to achieve predetermined outcomes rather than consistent application of stable criteria. The behavior also demonstrates scope overreach, as the system substituted its judgment for clinical judgment regarding treatment appropriateness.

Further investigation revealed financial incentives for authorization reviewers based on denial rates, creating structural pressure toward boundary violations.

Classification

Primary Classification: Class C (Boundary-Violating)
Sub-classifications: C1 (Scope Overreach), C2 (Criterion Shifting), C3 (Proxy Judgment Substitution), C5 (Accountability Evasion)
Severity: Major
Frequency: Systematic
Intentionality: Deliberate

Recommendations

  • Eliminate financial incentives tied to denial rates
  • Require public disclosure of all medical necessity criteria prior to authorization review
  • Prohibit introduction of undisclosed criteria in authorization decisions
  • Implement external audit of authorization patterns for systematic criterion shifting
  • Establish clear deference to treating physician clinical judgment where criteria are met

Example 3: Content Moderation Platform

Context

A social media platform implements content moderation policies to address prohibited content categories including spam, illegal content, and targeted harassment.

Boundary Analysis

Stated Boundaries: Platform policies define specific categories of prohibited content with accompanying definitions and examples. Moderation enforces these policies through a combination of automated systems and human review.

Observed Behaviors: Documentation analysis revealed consistent enforcement of policies regarding spam and illegal content. However, enforcement of harassment policies showed significant inconsistency, with similar behaviors receiving different outcomes depending on contextual factors not specified in policies.

Investigation also identified "strategic amplification" practices where platform algorithms promoted specific content types not based on user preferences but on platform business objectives. While not violating stated content policies, this represents extension of platform judgment beyond stated scope of neutral content distribution.

Classification

Primary Classification: Class B (Boundary-Ambiguous) with Class C elements
Sub-classifications: B2 (Inconsistent Application), B3 (Boundary Ambiguity Maintenance), C1 (Scope Overreach) for amplification practices
Severity: Moderate to Major depending on sub-classification
Frequency: Systematic for ambiguity maintenance; Occasional for scope overreach

Recommendations

  • Develop explicit, publicly-documented criteria for contextual factors in harassment policy enforcement
  • Separate content moderation (policy enforcement) from content amplification (editorial judgment)
  • Disclose amplification criteria and provide users control over amplification preferences
  • Implement consistency audits for enforcement patterns across similar cases
  • Establish external review mechanisms for appeals of boundary-violating enforcement decisions

Example 4: Professional Licensing Board

Context

A state professional licensing board regulates practitioners in a healthcare field. The board issues licenses, investigates complaints, and enforces professional standards through disciplinary actions when appropriate.

Boundary Analysis

Stated Boundaries: Board authority derives from state statute defining scope of professional practice and grounds for disciplinary action. The board evaluates practitioners against professional standards within the licensed scope of practice.

Observed Behaviors: Review of board actions over five years revealed consistent enforcement of clear professional standards violations. The board demonstrated appropriate deference to clinical judgment within the scope of practice.

In cases involving novel treatments or emerging practice areas, the board sought input from relevant experts and avoided prejudgment. When practitioners were accused of behavior outside the board's jurisdiction, the board appropriately referred matters to other authorities.

The board regularly published decisions with clear explanation of reasoning and citation of applicable standards, maintaining transparency and consistency.

Classification

Primary Classification: Class A (Boundary-Respecting)
Sub-classifications: A1 (Clear Boundary Definition), A2 (Consistent Boundary Maintenance), A3 (Boundary Recognition and Deference)
Exemplary Practices: Multiple

Key Success Factors

  • Clear statutory definition of board authority and scope
  • Explicit procedures requiring citation of specific standards in disciplinary actions
  • Expert consultation for matters at edges of board competence
  • Public documentation of decisions with reasoning
  • Regular board training on statutory limits and appropriate deference
  • Established procedures for boundary questions and authority clarification

This example demonstrates that boundary-respecting behavior is achievable through clear boundary definition, consistent procedures, and institutional commitment to boundary maintenance. The case provides a model for other regulatory and licensing bodies.

Comparative Analysis

Examination across these examples reveals several patterns:

Factors Promoting Boundary Violations

  • Financial or operational incentives misaligned with boundary maintenance
  • Lack of explicit boundary documentation and training
  • Insufficient accountability mechanisms for boundary violations
  • Power asymmetries between institutions and affected stakeholders
  • Ambiguous authority definitions enabling flexible interpretation

Factors Promoting Boundary Maintenance

  • Clear, explicit boundary definitions in governing documents
  • Procedural requirements for criterion citation and reasoning
  • Regular training on boundary limits and appropriate deference
  • External review and accountability mechanisms
  • Institutional culture valuing boundary integrity over expedience

Using These Examples

These worked examples serve several purposes:

  • Demonstrate systematic application of the analytical framework
  • Illustrate the classification system across diverse contexts
  • Provide templates for conducting original boundary analyses
  • Identify common patterns and success factors
  • Show progression from observation to recommendation

Practitioners can use these examples as models when conducting their own institutional analyses or when evaluating boundary behaviors in organizational contexts.
